## Context

Richfield, Minnesota — "The Urban Hometown" — is the state's oldest suburb, sitting directly south of Minneapolis. It has a real identity problem: the 2015 city logo that replaced a 30-year-old purple-and-blue badge was evaluated by the North American Vexillological Association and received an F grade. The welcome monument it was drawn from is more legible than either the flag or the logo.

The city's slogan is accurate — Richfield is genuinely urban-adjacent, walkable, diverse, and historically significant. The design challenge was building a system that finally said so.

The speculative brief: take the existing "Urban Hometown" positioning seriously, then rebuild the visual identity from the ground up using the city's actual design history and patterns rather than generic civic imagery.

## Approach

### Starting Point: The Existing Identity

The 2015 logo combines a skyline, suburban street lines, and nature motifs into a single mark — well-intentioned, but too literal and too complex to scale. The flag derived from it inherited all the same problems.

The rebrand doesn't reject the concept — *The Urban Hometown* is real and worth saying. It rejects the execution: too many ideas competing in one mark, no typographic backbone, no pattern system.
