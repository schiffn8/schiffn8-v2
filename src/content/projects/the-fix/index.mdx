## Context

The Fix is an overdose prevention campaign and intervention kit. It exists because I watched a friend die without naloxone in reach.

**Dedicated to Sawyer.**

{/* TODO — Write 1–2 sentences about the specific incident with Sawyer: when it happened, what the situation was, why not having naloxone on hand mattered. This is the emotional core of the project — the more specific you are, the more it lands. */}

Fentanyl contamination has made drug use unpredictably lethal — including for people who had no idea they were near it. Naloxone reverses opioid overdose in minutes, is available over the counter, and costs less than ten dollars. The problem isn't access. It's that most young people don't know what it is, don't carry it, and don't feel like the crisis applies to them.

The brief I set for myself: not just awareness, but intervention. A campaign that puts the knowledge and the tool in the same hands at the same moment.

This project began with [*High Enough?*](/work/high-enough) — a data visualization piece that mapped the scale of the epidemic. The Fix is what came after: the answer to what all that data means in practice.

## Approach

### Identity

The campaign anchors on yellow — high-visibility, universally readable as a signal, distinct from the clinical blue-white of pharmaceutical packaging. The voice is direct: *Be Prepared, Save Lives.* No euphemism.

### The Kit

The physical intervention kit pairs the campaign with action: naloxone, fentanyl test strips, and instructions in one package. The design challenge was making it feel approachable — something people would keep on them rather than stow in a drawer.
